Testing iPhone Screen Strength

Academic Project at the University of Pittsburgh

Details

This project was completed as a part of my Mechanical Measurements I course at the University of Pittsburgh. The goal was to create a test procedure which would measure the breakage strength of replacement iPhone screens. I was able to do so using strain gages, circuitry, and general engineering principles.

The driving principle that I decided to use was beam theory; I treated the iPhone screen as a simply supported beam. To test the actual strength of the screen, I dropped metal balls on it from varying heights. By assuming around 70% of the potential energy was absorbed by the beam, I determined the breakage strength using material properties, and the drop height, Z. The equation is shown below:



To further strengthen the analysis, consider the beam to be a spring, with a constant K. Using the drop height, you can determine the strain of the beam using Hooke's law. Using this method, I could physically record the strain (and stress) within the beam. This allowed me to compare my predicted strength from the previous section to the actual stress.

To measure the data, a DAQ was hooked up to the strain gauges. The gauges gave a change in voltage output, which was then run through a Wheatstone bridge, differential amplifier, and a low pass filter. This was to ensure the quality of the data being recorded. My circuit setup is shown below:



To further validate the analysis, I completed an detailed uncertainty analysis to determine the possible error within the results. Overall, the project was completed successfully and required knowledge from many different areas. I had to combine knowledge from circuits, data acquisition, beam theory, statics, and much more. I detemined that the beam had a breakage strength of around 400 [MPa], which is actually higher than the value specified by the manufacturer (350 [MPa]).
