Optical Sensor Cleaning Testbench

Senior Design Project Completed in Collaboration with Argo AI

Details

This project was completed during the final semester of my senior year (Spring 2021) at the University of Pittsburgh. A small team and I worked in collaboration with Argo AI.

Argo AI is a tech company that specializes in the development of autonomous vehicles. They work with both the advanced control algorithms as well as the physical hardware used to collect data about the carâ€™s surroundings. Argo AI is constantly pushing to develop a safer and more reliable autonomous vehicle. One of the ways that they aim to do so is by ensuring reliable sensor functionality. In particular, Argo AI was seeking to optimize cleaning techniques for different types of optical sensors. To do so, they needed a robust test setup which would allow them to evaluate cleaning techniques, such as changes in fluid solution, pressure, angle, etc. My group helped solve this problem by designing a test bench which could accommodate different types of sensors and allow for variations in cleaning techniques.

The test bench consisted of a adjustable 80/20 frame, with flexible nozzles attached. The adjustability of the frame allowed for the testing of sensors as big as ~12x12x16", and as small as ~2x2x2". The sensor was held by 2 steel beams that extended through the middle of the frame. The adjustable nozzles then were positioned around the sensor to test the effect of position/angles on the efficacy of cleaning. The nozzles were connected back to a pump system controlled via a microcontroller. We were able to configure the microcontroller to produce a PWM signal, which altered the pump pressure. This feature allowed us to control the velocity/pressure of the cleaning fluid.

We analyzed our design to evaluate risk in yielding and cost. A FMEA analysis was conducted to predict modes in which we could possibly break the test bench. Our main concerns included failure in the nozzle brackets and sensor mounting beam. We performed bending calculations for the beam, and a finite element analyze on the nozzle bracket. We found a high factor of safety for our design. For cost, we had a budget of $2,000 for each test bench. We were able to reduce our preliminary cost analyse of ~$1,900 to around ~$1,550 by increasing the ease of machining and replacing expensive parts.

Our most important goal was to create a highly adjustable design, while keeping the precision needed to run successful tests. We were successful in accomplishing this goal, as well as in creating a cost savings compared to initial targets. At the end of the term, we pitched our final design and prototype to Argo AI, and it was accepted.
